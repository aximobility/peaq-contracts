% === EXECUTIVE SUMMARY ===
\section{Executive summary}

AXI Mobility runs a fleet platform. AI agents do the work of monitoring vehicles, drivers, energy, commands, and risk; we turn those signals into cases, actions, and reports. Customer is the supervisor. AXI is the operator.

We built three things on peaq, all live on Agung testnet:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Insurance Risk Oracle} --- verifiable risk scores per vehicle and driver. Powers our 5--15\% premium-discount pitch to insurers.
  \item \textbf{High-Risk Command Vault} --- $K$-of-$M$ approval for fleet-wide commands (lock 47 bikes, mass route override).
  \item \textbf{Credential Revocation Registry} --- revocable driver licenses, NTSA inspections, insurance binders. Roadside inspectors and underwriters consult before underwriting.
\end{enumerate}

Plus a TypeScript SDK and standalone CLI that wraps peaq's three pallets (\texttt{peaqDid}, \texttt{peaqRbac}, \texttt{peaqStorage}), peaq EVM via viem, KMS-backed Ed25519 signers, and a daily Merkle anchor service.

\begin{successbox}
\textbf{Status today.} 51 unit and fuzz tests passing locally. Agung deploy dry-run successful. Pre-audit hardening checklist about 80\% complete. Production Agung deploy runbook ready (\texttt{AGUNG\_DEPLOY.md}). Mainnet promote is gated on Safe multisig setup and external audit acceptance.
\end{successbox}

% === THE THREE CONTRACTS ===
\section{What we built (the three use cases)}

Each contract solves a real problem we ran into during the ROAM pilot design.

\subsection{Insurance Risk Oracle}

\subsubsection{Problem}
Insurance underwriters in Kenya quote premiums based on patchy fleet data. They charge a "risk surcharge" because they cannot trust the numbers. ROAM (and every fleet operator we talked to) overpays by 15--25\%.

\subsubsection{Solution}
We attest a per-DID risk score on-chain. Append-only, signed by an authorised attestor. Score in $[0, 1000]$. Insurer reads the latest score before quoting. Verifiable. Auditable. Cheap.

\subsubsection{Real example}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=axilight,colframe=axiprimary,arc=2pt,boxrule=0.5pt]
ROAM rider Mary Akinyi has been riding 6 months, 4{,}200 km logged, zero harsh-brake events, 89\% safety score. AXI attestor signs:

{\small\ttfamily attest(did:peaq:mary, score=920, expiresAt=now+30d, payloadHash=0x...)}

Insurer reads \texttt{getLatest(did:peaq:mary)} and sees a score of 920 \enspace$\rightarrow$\enspace premium discount tier 1.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsubsection{Why peaq vs a centralised database}
Insurers will not trust AXI's database; we are an interested party. They will trust an on-chain attestation log signed by independent attestors. The chain gives them deniability ("we relied on a public register").

\subsubsection{Implementation}
\texttt{InsuranceRiskOracle.sol}: UUPS proxy, OpenZeppelin AccessControl, append-only history. Public read; role-gated write. \texttt{ATTESTOR\_ROLE} for AXI's attestation service; \texttt{CALIBRATOR\_ROLE} for the score-to-premium curve; \texttt{UPGRADER\_ROLE} for upgrades; \texttt{DEFAULT\_ADMIN\_ROLE} for role grants.

\subsection{High-Risk Command Vault}

\subsubsection{Problem}
Fleet-wide actions are risky. "Lock 47 bikes" is one button, and it has to be reversible, auditable, and multi-party. Operator dashboards routinely have a single-sig escape hatch --- that is how fleets get held ransom.

\subsubsection{Solution}
$K$-of-$M$ approval gate. AXI staff propose a high-risk command (lock fleet, mass route override, broadcast more than 100 messages, fund withdrawal). $M$ approvers must sign. Once $K$ reach (out of $M$), the command becomes executable by anyone --- including a regulator if AXI staff disappear.

\subsubsection{Real example}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=axilight,colframe=axiprimary,arc=2pt,boxrule=0.5pt]
Mary Akinyi's bike (KMG~117P) shows pack temperature 52$^\circ$C --- risk of fire. AXI ops Brian proposes \texttt{LOCK\_FLEET} on the 3 bikes in the same depot, $K=2$ of $M=3$.

Joy approves (1/2 reached). ROAM ops manager approves (2/2 reached). Vault status flips to \texttt{Approved}. Anyone calls \texttt{execute(commandId, payload)} \enspace$\rightarrow$\enspace bikes locked.

The on-chain audit shows: who proposed, who approved, when, and what.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsubsection{Why peaq vs an off-chain Slack approval}
A Slack approval can be repudiated. An on-chain $K$-of-$M$ cannot. The audit trail is the product --- insurance underwriters and regulators want to see that operators are practising good governance before they price the premium or licence the fleet.

\subsubsection{Implementation}
\texttt{HighRiskCommandVault.sol}: \emph{not} upgradeable (an upgradeable approval gate is a back door). OpenZeppelin AccessControl, ReentrancyGuard, Pausable. State machine \texttt{Proposed} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{Approved} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{Executed} (terminal). $1 \le K \le M \le 10$. Replay-safe \texttt{commandId} derivation. Permissionless execution once $K$ reached.

\subsection{Credential Revocation Registry}

\subsubsection{Problem}
Driver licenses, NTSA inspection certificates, and insurance binders all expire or get revoked. Today fleets find out at the roadside check (about \$300 fine plus a tow-truck bill). Insurers find out at claim time (about \$5{,}000 loss). Verification is paper-based and manual.

\subsubsection{Solution}
Each credential is hashed and registered. When the issuer (NTSA, insurance company, AXI fleet manager) revokes, they call \texttt{revoke(hash, reasonCode)}. Anyone --- insurer, regulator, the driver app itself --- can check \texttt{isRevoked(hash)} for free. Per-issuer scope: only the issuer of a credential can revoke it.

\subsubsection{Real example}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=axilight,colframe=axiprimary,arc=2pt,boxrule=0.5pt]
ROAM rider Wanjiku's NTSA inspection expires 2026-05-15. NTSA officer revokes:

{\small\ttfamily revoke(hash=0x... (Wanjiku's certificate), reasonCode=2 (expired))}

At the next checkpoint, the inspector scans the QR code on Wanjiku's bike. The verify app calls \texttt{isRevoked(hash)} and gets back \texttt{true}. Wanjiku is pulled aside, not jailed for "fraudulent docs". Saves AXI a court date.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsubsection{Why peaq vs a central NTSA database}
NTSA's portal goes down regularly. Our chain does not. Plus: insurance firms outside Kenya want to sell premium products to AXI fleets without trusting Kenya's central databases --- chain-anchored revocation gives them an independent verification path.

\subsubsection{Implementation}
\texttt{CredentialRevocationRegistry.sol}: UUPS proxy, OpenZeppelin AccessControl. Idempotent re-revoke (preserves the original \texttt{reasonCode}). Batch up to 100 revocations per transaction (handy when an issuer key is rotated). Emergency \texttt{pause()} for incidents.

\section{How it all fits together}

\subsection{The data path}

\begin{verbatim}
                                                     +-- Insurance underwriter
                                                     |
                                                     +-- Regulator (NTSA)
                                                     |
ROAM ops dashboard --+                               +-- Financier (lender)
                     |                               |
AXI Atlas Agent -----+--+ Convex Hub  --signs+anchors--> peaq mainnet (3338)
                     |                               |
Driver app ----------+                               +-- Vault (K-of-M)
                                                     +-- Risk Oracle (append-only)
                                                     +-- Revocation Registry
                                                     |
                                                     +-- peaqDid / peaqRbac / peaqStorage
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{Three flows, every day}

\subsubsection{Flow A --- Daily Merkle anchor (00:00 UTC)}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Convex cron walks yesterday's \texttt{auditLogs} (every state change is hash-chained).
  \item Builds a Merkle tree (binary, SHA-256 leaves).
  \item Calls \texttt{anchor(dayId, root)} on \textbf{peaq mainnet} primary plus \textbf{Polygon mainnet} fallback.
  \item \texttt{peaqscan}, Subscan, and Polygonscan all show the same root.
  \item Anyone can pick a random event from yesterday, walk the proof, and verify.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Flow B --- Per-event critical hash (live)}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item ATL agent flags a high-risk event (siphoning, collision, geofence breach in restricted zone).
  \item Event is hashed and signed Ed25519 by AXI's KMS-backed signer (the production key never leaves KMS).
  \item Tx submitted to peaq mainnet \emph{immediately} (not waiting for the daily anchor).
  \item The case in our cloud dashboard shows the chain TX link as a "verified" pill.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Flow C --- Public verification (on demand)}
Insurance underwriter holds a fleet's risk-attestation hash. They call our \texttt{verify.aximobility.com/api/verify/[hash]} endpoint. We return the Merkle proof plus the on-chain TX. They walk the proof against the chain themselves. No trust in AXI required.

\subsection{Pallet vs EVM split (decision rationale)}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{DID + Storage + RBAC} use \emph{pallets} --- peaq's native primitives, cheaper, type-safe via Substrate.
  \item \textbf{Vault + Oracle + Registry} use \emph{EVM contracts} --- rich state machines plus UUPS upgradability plus audit firms know Solidity.
  \item \textbf{Daily anchor} uses EVM --- tooling plus Polygon redundancy on the same artefact.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Direct answers to seven unverified questions}

These come from \texttt{VERIFIED\_SPECS.md}. We want to publish answers in that file as "verified, source: peaq team confirmation".

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Exact extrinsic fees.} What does \texttt{peaqDid.addAttribute} cost in PEAQ and USD? What about \texttt{peaqStorage.addItem}? We need this for capacity planning and cost-of-anchor model.

  \item \textbf{Major incidents and forks since mainnet launch.} For our risk model.
